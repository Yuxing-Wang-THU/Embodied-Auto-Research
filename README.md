# Embodied-Auto-Research

A long-standing goal in embodied intelligence is to design agents whose bodies and controllers are jointly optimized for their tasks and environments. The morphology of a robot determines what physical interactions are possible, while the controller determines how those physical affordances are exploited. This coupling makes morphology--controller co-design a difficult problem: a morphology that performs poorly early in training may be intrinsically flawed, or it may simply require more controller adaptation; conversely, a high-performing controller may mask structural inefficiencies in the body. Distinguishing these cases is essential for effective embodied co-design.

Existing approaches to robot co-design largely treat this problem as a black-box optimization problem. Evolutionary methods search over body structures and controllers simultaneously, while reinforcement learning based methods optimize morphology and control policies using task rewards. Although these methods can produce high-performing agents, they typically provide limited insight into why a design works, which modules contribute to performance, or when morphology should be changed rather than allowing the controller to continue learning. This lack of diagnosis becomes especially problematic under finite environment interaction budgets, where unnecessary morphology changes can disrupt controller learning and waste valuable training steps.

Large language models (LLMs) and vision-language models (VLMs) offer a new opportunity for robot design. Recent work has used foundation models to generate robot morphologies, propose design variations, or assist in task-conditioned design. However, most existing LLM-based robot design pipelines use language models primarily as **generators**: they propose an initial morphology or a set of candidate designs, after which optimization is delegated to conventional search or reinforcement learning algorithms. In this paradigm, the LLM does not continuously participate in the embodied design process. It does not observe how the robot behaves, diagnose why certain modules succeed or fail, or decide whether future effort should be spent on controller learning, morphology adaptation, or measurement refinement.

We argue for a different view: large models can serve as **embodied researchers** in the robot co-design loop. Rather than merely generating designs, they can help construct diagnostic instruments, interpret behavioral evidence, formulate design hypotheses, and decide how to allocate limited experimentation budget. We refer to this broader direction as **Embodied Auto Research (EAR)**: a paradigm in which foundation models participate in closed-loop embodied experimentation by iteratively probing, diagnosing, and adapting robot designs.
